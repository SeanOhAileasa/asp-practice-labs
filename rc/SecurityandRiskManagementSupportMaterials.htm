Supporting Lab Material - Security and Risk Management Part 1

Security management encompasses the administrative, technical and physical controls necessary to adequately protect the confidentiality, integrity, and availability which is known as the CIA Triad of information assets.

Regardless of the size of an organization, security management is an essential task for IT professionals to ensure that risks are identified beforehand, and consequently, controls or restrictions are applied to minimize its impact on the business.

Security management ensures the interrelationships among assessing risk, implementing policies and controls in response to the risks, promoting awareness of the expectations, monitoring the effectiveness of the controls, and using the collected information as a reference point for the next risk assessment.

Confidentiality, Integrity, and Availability

Confidentiality

Confidentiality aims to prevent information being seen by unauthorized people.

  • Ensures that only authorized people are able to access data
  • Access controls
  • Encryption

Examples of attacks:

  • Journalists paying for information
  • Hackers compromising systems to sell personal information and credit card numbers

Integrity

Integrity involves maintaining the consistency, accuracy, and trustworthiness of the information over its entire lifecycle.

  • Prevents the unauthorized modification of data while in transit and ensures that unauthorized modification is detected
  • Access controls and audit logging

Examples of attacks:

  • Changes to log files
  • Changes to permissions

Availability

The availability covers the information with respect to ensuring that the information is available whenever it is needed.

  • Ensures that systems and data are available when needed
  • Protection against threats
  • Backups
  • Fault tolerance and redundancies to ensure high availability

Examples of attacks:

  • Software crashes or bugs
  • Data deletions
  • Denial of Service attack

Safeguarding data includes analyzing the risks, threats and the controls required to secure information using accepted security standards like strong passwords, security awareness, account lockouts, data backups, etc. against social engineering, man-in-the-middle attacks, and numerous other attack formats.

Other important concepts include:

Identification, Authentication, Authorization, Auditing, Accountability, Nonrepudiation.

Apply Security Governance Principles

Most importantly is the need to design a system which aligns with the business strategy, goals, mission and the business objectives. This requires a strong and well-written security policy which is adhered to.

Often, a top-down approach is used to design the security needs where the key management is responsible for initiating and designing the policy structure implemented by different working groups within the business. For example, the software development team with the design of a custom application and consequently reviewed by a security professional who will write the documentation and endorse remediation to patch loopholes within the application.

Often this will follow 3 types of a plan known as:

  1. Strategic Plan: Long-term which is stable in context and covers goals, missions and organization objectives usually valid for about 5 years.
  2. Tactical Plan: Midterm which has clearer details regarding scheduling of activities, the hiring of staff, budgets, maintenance, and support. This is valid for about a year in length.
  3. Operational Plan: Short-term plan which is highly detailed and documents granular processes which include detailed budgets, staffing assignments, scheduling, and implementation procedures.

Change management

Without a good change control process, gaps can appear in security measures such as loopholes, overlaps missing objects and oversights. A planning process should include documented and audited planning, testing, logging, auditing and monitoring of activities related to security controls.

The idea is that any changes which happened can indeed be rolled back should it become apparent that the change had a negative effect on the business and/or security.

Data classification

Data is typically considered against its secrecy, sensitivity or confidentiality. The process involves organizing objects, items, and subjects into groups or categories which have defined security roles and rules to prohibit and control how information is being accessed and by whom. Often the different standards will involve the usefulness, value (cost), maturity, national security, restriction, and storage to name a few of the used concepts.

After which information will be given classifications of differing levels such as Confidential, Private, Sensitive and Public or Top Secret, Secret, Confidential and Unclassified. It’s also important to list out the relevant security roles and the responsibilities of those who will be assuming said roles. These need to be quite prescriptive in nature. Common roles include Senior Manager, Security Professional, Data Owner, Data Custodian, User, and Auditor.

The exercise of due care describes what legal duty is considered to be whereas a lack of due care is termed as negligence.

The application of due diligence is a pre-emptive measure taken to avoid harm to other persons or their property.

Compliance and Frameworks

Compliance refers to actions that ensure the organization complies with rules, laws as well the organization's policies.

Governance, Risk Management and Compliance (GRC)

Governance, risk management, and compliance manage risks in line with business and compliance objectives. Governance ensures that the business focuses on the core goals and objectives and defines who in the organization has the authority to make decisions, who is accountable, and responsible for the outcomes. It also addresses how expected performance will be evaluated.

Legislative and regulatory compliance

Organizations must operate under the laws, regulations, and compliance requirements within the country and the industry, and security professionals must understand these.

Privacy requirements compliance

Personally, identifiable information is becoming an extremely valuable commodity for both legal and illegal entities where data protection is essential to private information. There is a huge growth in social networking sites which allows individuals to expose huge quantities of personal information, while this is useful, it is also valuable information, such as their personal locations which can be live updates, where they are traveling and what new personal items they may have acquired through purchases.

IS027001 is a key piece of the framework used and recognized around the world as a basis for security practices. It’s the international standard that describes best practice for an information security management system (ISMS).

It is designed to serve as a single reference point for identifying the range of controls needed for most situations where information systems are used.

ISMS Family of Standards

  • ISO/IEC 27001:2013
  • ISO/IEC 27002:2013
  • ISO/IEC 20000-1:2011
  • ISO/IEC 27013:2012

If security around credit card payment systems is required, then most commonly companies seek out:

  • PCI DSS - Standard to be followed for credit card payment systems.

To ensure that the security and privacy requirements are met, most businesses adopt control frameworks to provide governance that is:

  • Consistent - In approach and how it is applied.
  • Measurable - Provide a way to determine progress and set goals.
  • Standardized - Results should be comparable in a meaningful way.
  • Comprehensive - Cover the minimum legal and regulatory requirements and be extensible to accommodate additional specific requirements.
  • Modular - The ability to overcome changes and assist in organizing data for compliance.

Examples include:

  • NIST - American bound guidelines which are very thorough
  • COBIT - A methodology assisting with security audits and risk management
  • ITIL - A methodology assisting with audits and processes

Legal and Regulatory Issues

The first difference where the law is concerned is the idea of what criminal law is and what common law refers to.

Criminal Law - Body of laws which aim at preserving peace and safety in society. The punishment for breaking these laws are often fines, imprisonment and community service.

Common Law / Civil Law - Laws which refer to matters other than crimes such as contract disputes, employment or estate matters. These can also result in criminal law punishments.

Administrative Law/ Public Law - These refer to activities around crafting of rules or enforcement of regulations, duties, and rights of public bodies.

The laws themselves differ in terms of interpretation, implementation and exact wording between countries. To address these differences, nations have international agreements like extradition treaties to prosecute criminals and lawbreakers. However, on the whole, there are a number of laws between countries that have the same aim.

Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (Explained) - A crime is committed when:

Access to classified and financial information without permission, even more so if the device belongs to the government.

Using a device to commit fraud or cause malicious damage to a computer system, application or another.

Modifying medical information on the computer which results in the loss or impairment of examination, diagnosis, treatment or care of an individual.

Therefore, when it comes to compliance on an international scale, following security Standards such as the Payment Card Industry Data Security Standard or PCI DSS is a good idea. These are not laws but are standards used to comply with regulations by observing what is considered good security practice and that which meets the regulations of that country. By meeting the PCI-DSS in a contractual agreement, the business can demonstrate that they are enforcing controls against financial fraud for example.

Code of Ethics

In simplest terms, a code of ethics is a set of principles that serves two key purposes:

  • Each individual must conduct business honestly
  • Each individual must conduct business with integrity.

Most organizations have a code of ethics document, which contains the mission and values of the organization.

Develop and implement documented Security Policy, Standards, Procedures, and Guidelines

Security Policies

This document is used to define the scope of security required by the business; it includes which assets are to be considered and how far a security solution should be implemented, it holds the security objectives and framework outline. This forms the strategic plan and also begins the assignment of roles, audit requirements, acceptable risk levels, and responsibilities.

In general, there are three forms of policy which are used namely:

  • Regulatory -This ensures that the organization is following standards set by specific industry regulations (HIPAA, GLBA, PCI-DSS).
  • Advisory -This advises employees as to which types of behaviors and activities should and should not take place within the organization.
  • Informative - This is not an enforceable policy, rather it teaches individuals about specific issues relevant to the company.

Security Standards, Baselines, Guidelines, and Procedures

Standards are mandatory activities, actions, or rules. They give a policy some support and reinforcement in direction. Organizational security standards may specify how hardware and software products should be used and expected user behavior. This acts to ensure that specific technologies, applications, parameters, and procedures are implemented in a uniform (standardized) manner across the organization.

Baselining refers to forming a comparison point for future changes resulting in a consistent reference point. Once risks have been mitigated and security put in place, a baseline is formally reviewed and agreed upon, after which all further comparisons and development are measured against it.

Guidelines are recommended actions and operational instructions for users, IT and operations staff or others when a specific standard does not apply. Guidelines map out the methodologies of technology, personnel, or physical security.

Procedures are used to present detailed step-by-step actions for the same staff members. For example, install operating systems, configuring security devices, implementing access control lists on routers, setting up new user accounts, assigning computer privileges.

Business Continuity Requirements

There are four key steps to observe:

  • Project scope and planning
  • Business impact assessment
  • Continuity planning
  • Approval and implementation

The Project Scope and Planning

An analysis is performed of the resources, legal and regulatory requirements around the business by a newly formed business continuity planning (BCP) team that has senior management approval.

The BCP team consists of representatives from different departments such as security, finance, IT and legal departments. The team then works to establish the plan Development Testing, Training and Maintenance, and Implementation.

Contribute to Personnel Security Policies

Security policies related to personnel need to contain clear guidelines on working with not only a variety of situations in recruitment but also working on maintaining security checks on current employees and should to deal with contractors who enter the business as 3rd party workers.

Employment candidate screening

Checks are first typically performed against the job role and organization security to ensure that qualified and suitable.

Standard checks used:

  • Reference checks
  • Background checks
  • Criminal record checks
  • Counter-terrorist checks
  • Prior employment
  • Credit history

Other essential checks for suitability:

  • Driving records
  • Drug tests
  • ID checks
  • Education

Employment agreements and policies

Job rotation is used to reduce the risk of collusion between individuals, whereas least privilege allows users only access to functions required to perform their job role. Often the application of mandatory vacations/holidays on specified consecutive-day periods help to break up job boredom and monotony. Finally, separation of duties prevents one individual from performing all activities single-handedly, for example, involving senior management’s input for creating the job description helps draw the line where an employee responsibility begins and ends.

Employment termination processes

Regular termination is a standard procedure where an amicable agreement between two parties (employer and employee) has been reached, and company property is returned with an exit interview.

Unfriendly terminations result from an individual being fired, involuntarily transferred or laid off maybe for gross misconduct or breach of contract.

The vendor, consultant, and contractor controls

Business partners or third parties often subcontract and need personnel to visit locations, as such a screening committee helps to prevent the risk of information loss. Third parties who are on site or accessing systems should be escorted with specific monitoring in mind. If the site is high security implemented, then an Enhanced non-disclosure document should be signed.

Regular or permanent third parties onsite will have to undergo comparative screening as well as specific monitoring and signing an enhanced non-disclosure document.

Compliance

To ensure the organization complies with established rules as well as providing the tools to verify that compliance, and includes adherence with laws as well the organization’s policies.

Privacy

Typically, most individuals expect a level of privacy both at work and in private life. Therefore, a security professional must respect how information is monitored especially when different jurisdictions are concerned.

As an example, most people agree to CCTV cameras placed on front door entrances but not within personal environments such as a toilet or shower.

Apply Risk Management Concepts

Definition:

“Risk is a function of the likelihood of a given threat source’s exercising a particular potential vulnerability and the resulting impact of that adverse event on the organization”-NIST SP 800-30.

Risk management - systematic application of management policies, procedures and practices to the activities of communicating, consulting, establishing the context and identifying, analyzing, evaluating, treating, monitoring and reviewing risk.

Threats exploit vulnerabilities creating risk.

Risk management is the process of identifying, assessing, and prioritizing threats and risks. Risk assessments are used to identify the risks that might impact your particular environment.

  • Risk is generally defined as the probability that an event will occur.
  • A threat, which is defined as an action or occurrence that could result in the breach, outage, or corruption of a system by exploiting known or unknown vulnerabilities.
  • The goal of any risk management plan is to remove risks when possible and to minimize the consequences of risks that cannot be eliminated.

NIST Guidelines for Risk Assessment

  • Determine scope and methodology
  • Identify assets and the systems to be analyzed
  • Collect and analyze
  • Asset valuation
  • Consequence assessment
  • Threat identification
  • Safeguard analysis
  • Vulnerability analysis
  • Likelihood assessment
  • Interpret risk analysis results
  • Acceptance and implementation of cost-effective controls

Risk Estimation

In the table below the following key should be observed:

L - Low Risk

M - Medium Risk

H - High Risk

E - Extreme Risk

By applying a matrix, the risk levels become associated with cells which assist when making decisions based on control expenditure and explaining the severity of events to senior management.

Monetary vales used in calculation of risk

AV = asset value

EF = Exposure Factor - loss of value if threat instantiated

(% of the asset value = 20% or 0.2)

SLE = Single Loss Expectancy

        = AV x EF

ARO = Annualised Rate of Occurrence

(twice a year = 2, once every 5 years = 0.2)

ALE = Annualised Loss Expectancy

          = ARO x SLE

Example - Server Unit - Power Surge causes damage

  • AV = £10000 value
  • EF = 20% loss if exposure happens
  • SLE = AV x EF = £2000 value
  • ARO = once every 5 years = 0.2
  • SLE = £2000 (as above)
  • ALE = ARO x SLE= 0.2 x £2000 = £400

Risk Mitigation and Controls

Risk has four essential control groups, namely:

  1. Risk reduction - Controls are used to reduce the likelihood/impact of the risk or minimise loss.
  2. Risk avoidance - Removing the threat by using alternate behavior to end the exposure.
  3. Risk transfer - Pass risk onto 3rd party such as an insurance company.
  4. Risk retention/acceptance - accepting the risk outcome as necessary to continuing the business goals and confirming monetary funds are available if required.

The controls are classified as Directive, deterrent, preventive, compensating, detective, corrective, recovery. Example of which include:

  1. Technical controls - Authentication, access control, encryption, etc.
  2. Procedural controls - Security awareness, training, etc.
  3. Physical controls - Fences, doors, fire suppression, etc.
  4. Legal, regulatory and compliance - Security policies, regulations, etc.

Apply Threat Modeling

Threat modeling aims first to understand how the business assets could be attractive to nefarious activities from either external or internal actors. Then work to reduce the threat surface of the business model by implementing proactive and reactive countermeasures.

A common term used is to Secure by Design, Secure by Default, Secure in Deployment and Communication.

Threats are reviewed considering their probability of occurrence, impact levels, the priority of concerns or threat reduction methods. This considers the assets, attackers and software, the hardware of the business.

The analysis will then form a “defense in depth” which means using multiple layers of security to defend your assets. 

That way, even if an attacker breaches one layer of your defense, you have additional layers to keep that person out of the critical areas of your environment where the outer defenses protect internally held assets. If outer defenses are breached - inner defenses must be strong enough to protect the assets.

These protection mechanisms, therefore, provide layering, abstraction, data hiding, and encryption.

Typical threats to be considerate of immediately are Spoofing, Tampering, Repudiation, Information Disclosure, Denial of Service and Elevation of Privileges.

These form the acronym of STRIDE. This provides the first stage in reviewing the assets against threats by considering how easily an asset is manipulated by STRIDE actors. Flow diagrams help to demonstrate the processes and defenses which are in place visually.

Once clarified, the next logical step is to perform reduction analysis or to decompose the application, system or environment in an effort to gain a great understanding of the interactions with all the elements involved when the threat exercises actions.

Here the auditing checking for Trust Boundaries, Data Flow Paths, Input Paths, Privileged Operations, and Security Stand and Approach. Once conclusions are drawn, it’s time to list the priorities and Responses using DREAD or Damage Potential, Reproducibility, Exploitability, Affected Users and Discoverability.

Integrate security risk considerations into acquisition strategy and practice

The purchasing of equipment, software, and staff is essential to risk management strategies, as an incorrect asset could alter the risk evaluation significantly. However, resilient integrated security options are more expensive than assets which provide very straightforward solutions.

It’s important to consider the lifetime of the asset, the total cost of ownership including maintenance fees, the elements of the contractual agreement and how they will be enforced. Software needs constant monitoring to make sure it's behaving correctly and to the designed specifications.

If using contractors, then the security clearance of that personnel need to be understood.

  1. On-Site Assessment - where staff is interviewed and observed.
  2. Document Exchange and Review - How datasets and documentation are exchanged securely.
  3. Process/Policy Review - Copies of security policies should be readily available for review.
  4. Where a process is non-existent, then this should be modeled from the business security policy to establish a minimum level of acceptance.

Establish and manage information security education, training, and awareness

A security professional should assist with formal security awareness training as a method to inform employees about their roles, and expectations surrounding their roles, with regards to information security. This helps to inform about a particular security or risk management function while achieving regulatory and legislative compliance.

Further, there should be training lessons based on the different business functions and the risks. For example, a lesson related to the finance department from monetary fraud attempts, the Human Resources department regarding personal information and phishing attempts and higher management regarding spear phishing attacks.

Awareness aims at bringing security to the forefront by raising its importance to all staff members not just the IT security team. Security awareness training can be achieved through screensavers, posters, announcements, as well as courses.

Training helps employees understand how to comply with security while performing their job role.
